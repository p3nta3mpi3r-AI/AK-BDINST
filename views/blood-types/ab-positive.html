Quick Facts

Prevalence in Oklahoma ~3% of population
Red cells can donate to AB+ only
Plasma can donate to ALL 8 blood types (universal plasma donor)
Can receive from All 8 blood types (universal recipient)
Best donation type Plasma or platelets
AB positive (AB+) is unique in two ways: AB+ individuals are universal recipients for red blood cells (they can receive from any blood type) AND AB+ is the universal plasma donor (AB+ plasma can be given to any patient regardless of blood type). With only about 3% of Oklahomans having AB+, this dual distinction makes AB+ plasma donations especially critical.
Because AB+ red blood cells can only be given to AB+ patients (who are rare), whole blood donation from AB+ donors has limited reach. However, AB+ plasma — the liquid component of blood — can be transfused to any patient in any blood type emergency. This is why Oklahoma Blood Institute specifically recruits AB+ donors for plasma donations.
AB+ donors are encouraged to donate plasma as their primary contribution. Plasma is used in emergency trauma care, to treat immune deficiencies, and to produce life-saving medications. AB+ plasma means a single AB+ donor can help any patient in the hospital — the ultimate in donation reach. AB+ plasma donors can give up to twice per week.

Common Myths About AB+ Blood

Myth: "AB+ donors can't give to many people, so it's less important to donate."

Fact: AB+ plasma is the only truly universal plasma type. AB+ plasma donors can help patients of any blood type, making their plasma uniquely valuable in emergencies.

Myth: "Being a universal recipient means you shouldn't bother donating."

Fact: What you can receive doesn't affect what you can give. AB+ plasma donors make a massive positive impact regardless of how many types could help them.

Your AB+ plasma can help any patient — the most versatile donation in the blood supply.

Frequently Asked Questions

AB+ plasma is the universal plasma type — it can be given to any patient regardless of blood type. It's used in trauma emergencies, to treat clotting disorders, immune deficiencies, and to manufacture medications like IVIG and clotting factor concentrates.

Plasma can be donated up to twice per week. Whole blood: every 56 days. Platelets: up to 24 times per year. Plasma donation is the highest-impact option for most AB+ donors.

AB− is the rarest (~1%), followed by B− (~2%), then AB+ (~3%). AB+ is uncommon but AB− is rarer.
