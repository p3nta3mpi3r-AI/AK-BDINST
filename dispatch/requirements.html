Oklahoma Blood Donation Requirements: Everything You Need to Know to Donate

If you want to donate blood in Oklahoma, this page has every eligibility rule in one place — age, weight, hemoglobin, ID, frequency limits, common medical conditions, and what to bring on the day. When in doubt, your nearest OBI donor center has final say.

The five rules that matter most
  1. Age and weight: 16 years old with signed parental consent (125 lb minimum); 17 years old (125 lb minimum, no consent form needed); 18 or older (110 lb minimum).
  2. Photo ID required at every visit — driver's license, state ID, student ID, or passport.
  3. Generally good health: you should feel well on donation day, with no fever, active infection, or illness symptoms.
  4. Hemoglobin: at least 12.5 g/dL for women and 13.0 g/dL for men — a 30-second finger-stick test checks this on arrival.
  5. No recent illness or medications of concern: if you're on blood thinners or antibiotics, or have been sick in the last 24-48 hours, check with your center before you come in.

The 5-Minute Pre-Screen: Can You Donate Today?

Work through these questions in order. A "No" at any step means you should call your center before coming in. "Yes" through all five means you're most likely good to go.

Step 1 — Age and weight
Are you at least 16 years old AND do you meet the weight requirement for your age?

Yes, keep going. If you're 16 or 17, you need to weigh at least 125 lb. If you're 18 or older, 110 lb is the minimum. If you're 16, also confirm you have a signed parental consent form.

No or unsure: You may not be eligible yet. Call your nearest center — they can confirm requirements and help you plan ahead for when you qualify.

Step 2 — Do you feel well today?
Are you free of fever, active cold or flu symptoms, sore throat, and active infections?

Yes, keep going. You don't need to be in peak athletic condition — just feeling basically normal. A mild, stable chronic condition like well-controlled diabetes or managed high blood pressure is fine.

No: Wait until you have been symptom-free for at least 24-48 hours. If you are on antibiotics, wait until you have finished the course and feel well again.

Step 3 — Medications
Are you NOT currently taking blood thinners (warfarin, heparin, Xarelto, Eliquis), isotretinoin (Accutane), or finasteride for prostate?

Yes (not on those medications), keep going. Most common medications are fine. Blood pressure pills, metformin, antidepressants, cholesterol medications, and thyroid hormones do not disqualify you.

No (you are on one of those medications): Check with your center. Some require a waiting period after your last dose; others are permanent deferrals depending on the reason they were prescribed.

Step 4 — Recent tattoos, piercings, or international travel
In the last 3 months, did you get a tattoo or piercing at an unregulated facility, OR travel to a malaria-risk country?

No, keep going. Tattoos and piercings from licensed Oklahoma facilities carry no waiting period. Most domestic and mainstream international destinations are fine.

Yes: A 3-month waiting period may apply. Call your center to confirm based on your specific travel destination or where you had the work done.

Step 5 — Do you have photo ID?
Can you bring a valid photo ID to your appointment?

Yes — you're ready to donate. Driver's license, state ID, student ID, passport, or military ID all work. Bring it to every visit.

No: You cannot donate without ID. Get a valid photo ID before booking — Oklahoma DPS can issue a state ID card even without a driver's license.

What to Bring on Donation Day

  • Photo ID — driver's license, state ID, student ID, passport, or military ID. Required every visit, no exceptions.
  • Your medication list — write down everything you take, including over-the-counter drugs, vitamins, and supplements. Health screeners need this information.
  • Comfortable, loose clothing — wear short sleeves or a sleeve that rolls up easily above the elbow. You'll be sitting or reclining for 10 minutes to over an hour, depending on donation type.
  • A snack for afterward — you'll get a snack and juice at the center, but bringing something extra (crackers, a granola bar, fruit) helps with recovery if you tend to feel lightheaded.
  • Water — aim to drink an extra 16 oz of water in the hours before your appointment. Proper hydration makes the donation faster and easier, and reduces the chance of feeling dizzy afterward.
  • Parental consent form (age 16 only) — download from the donor center's website, fill it out with a parent or guardian, and bring the signed form with you.

What to Expect: Step by Step

  1. Registration (5-10 minutes)

    When you arrive, a staff member will check your photo ID, confirm your contact information, and enter you into the system. First-time donors fill out a brief registration form. Return donors are usually already in the system from their last visit. You'll also receive a donor number that tracks your donation history and blood type results going forward.

  2. Health screening (10-15 minutes)

    A trained screener takes you through a private health history questionnaire covering travel, medications, illnesses, and lifestyle factors. They'll also take your blood pressure, pulse, temperature, and a quick finger-stick to check your hemoglobin level. Everything you share is confidential. Answer all questions honestly — this protects you and the patients who receive your blood.

  3. The donation itself (8-10 minutes for whole blood; longer for platelets or Power Red)

    A phlebotomist will clean a small area on your inner arm, insert a sterile needle (used once and discarded), and draw your blood into a collection bag. For whole blood, this takes about 8-10 minutes. Platelet and Power Red donations use an apheresis machine that separates components and can take 60-90 minutes. Most donors read, watch TV, or scroll their phones during this time.

  4. Post-donation rest (10-15 minutes)

    After donating, you'll move to a refreshment area where you can sit, have a snack and juice, and let your body begin recovering. Staff will check on you and watch for any signs of lightheadedness or nausea. Most donors feel completely normal within 10-15 minutes. Do not rush this step — it is the most important one for preventing post-donation dizziness.

  5. Aftercare (rest of the day)

    Keep the bandage on for at least 4-6 hours. Avoid heavy lifting or strenuous exercise for the rest of the day. Continue drinking water. If you feel dizzy after leaving, sit or lie down immediately — do not drive until you feel fully normal. Your body replaces plasma within 24 hours; red blood cells take 4-6 weeks to fully replenish, which is why the 56-day whole blood wait exists.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I donate blood if I'm 16 in Oklahoma?

Yes. 16-year-olds can donate whole blood in Oklahoma with a signed parental consent form and a minimum weight of 125 pounds. Bring your photo ID and the completed, signed consent form to your appointment. Some donation types (platelets, Power Red) may have additional requirements for minors — confirm with your center.

What's the minimum weight to donate blood in Oklahoma?

If you're 16 or 17, you must weigh at least 125 pounds. If you're 18 or older, the minimum is 110 pounds. For some donation types such as Power Red, there are additional weight and height combination requirements — your center screener will walk you through those if applicable.

How often can I donate blood in Oklahoma?

Whole blood: every 56 days (8 weeks). Platelets: every 7 days, up to 24 times per year. Power Red: every 112 days. Plasma: approximately every 28 days, depending on center scheduling. Your donation record is tracked automatically so the center will flag you if you come in too soon.

Do I need an appointment to donate?

Walk-ins are accepted at most Oklahoma donor centers, but an appointment means less wait time, guaranteed equipment availability for specialty donations like platelets or Power Red, and a smoother experience for everyone. Booking online usually takes under two minutes.

Will I know my blood type after donating?

Yes. Oklahoma's nonprofit blood centers test every donation and send you your blood type — usually within 1-2 weeks by mail, email, or through a donor portal. If this is your first donation, you'll also learn whether your blood is CMV-negative, which is especially valuable for premature infant care.

What hemoglobin level do I need to donate blood?

The minimum hemoglobin is 12.5 g/dL for women and 13.0 g/dL for men. A quick finger-stick test done at the center during health screening checks this in about 30 seconds. If your levels are too low, you'll be deferred temporarily — not permanently. Eating iron-rich foods (meat, beans, leafy greens, fortified cereals) in the days before your appointment can help keep levels up.

Can I donate blood if I have a tattoo or piercing?

In Oklahoma, tattoos and piercings from licensed, state-regulated facilities carry no waiting period. You can donate the same day if everything else checks out. If you had a tattoo or piercing at an unregulated facility, or at an out-of-state studio where you can't confirm regulation status, a 3-month waiting period applies. Oklahoma is one of the more donor-friendly states on this rule because it maintains a robust tattoo facility licensing program.

Can I donate if I take blood pressure medication or other prescription drugs?

Most prescription medications do not disqualify you. Blood pressure medications, metformin, antidepressants, cholesterol drugs, and thyroid hormones are all generally acceptable. Bring a complete medication list to every appointment and disclose everything during health screening. Medications that typically cause deferral include warfarin, heparin, novel oral anticoagulants (Eliquis, Xarelto), and isotretinoin (Accutane). Your screener makes the final call based on your full medication history.

Can I donate blood if I had COVID-19?

Yes, with a waiting period. You must be fully recovered, symptom-free, and have completed any isolation requirements before donating. If you were treated with certain monoclonal antibody therapies, a longer deferral may apply. Check with your center for the current standard, as guidelines have been updated periodically since 2020.

What happens if I get deferred — does that mean I can never donate?

Most deferrals are temporary. Common temporary reasons include low hemoglobin, recent illness, a new tattoo from an unregulated facility, or recent travel to a malaria-risk region. Permanent deferrals are rare and typically involve specific medical histories that put recipients at risk. If you're deferred, ask your screener exactly when you can try again and what steps, if any, you can take to improve your eligibility.
