How to Prepare

What to Eat Before Donating Blood

Short answer: Eat a full, iron-rich meal two to four hours before your appointment, and drink at least 16 extra ounces of water. The two most common reasons donations go sideways — lightheadedness and a failed hemoglobin check — are both largely preventable with good pre-donation nutrition and hydration. Avoid high-fat foods and alcohol for at least 24 hours before donating.

What to Eat, What to Avoid, What to Drink

Eat: iron-rich foods (start a few days before)
  • Lean red meat: beef, lamb, venison
  • Poultry: chicken, turkey
  • Fish and shellfish — especially clams, oysters, and tuna
  • Beans and lentils: kidney beans, black beans, chickpeas, lentils
  • Tofu and edamame
  • Dark leafy greens: spinach, Swiss chard, kale
  • Iron-fortified breakfast cereals (check the label for 100% daily value)
  • Pumpkin seeds and sunflower seeds

Pair iron foods with vitamin C to boost absorption — squeeze lemon on your spinach, drink orange juice with breakfast, or add tomatoes or bell peppers to your meal.

Eat on donation day: balanced, not heavy
  • Whole grain bread, toast, oatmeal, or rice — complex carbs give you sustained energy
  • Eggs, lean chicken, or turkey — protein without high fat
  • Fresh fruit: bananas, oranges, berries
  • Low-fat yogurt

Eat your meal two to four hours before your appointment — recent enough to keep blood sugar stable, far enough ahead that digestion is underway.

Avoid: high-fat foods (day of donation)
  • Fried food: french fries, fried chicken, donuts
  • Burgers, pizza, or meals with heavy cheese or cream sauces
  • Full-fat dairy in large amounts: butter, cream, full-fat ice cream
  • Chips, crackers with butter, pastries

High-fat meals cause lipemia — elevated fat particles in the blood — which clouds the plasma and can cause the donation to be discarded during testing. This is a quality issue, not a safety issue, but it wastes your donation.

Avoid: alcohol and excess caffeine
  • No alcohol for at least 24 hours before donating — it dehydrates you and increases dizziness risk
  • Limit caffeine to one cup; prioritize water instead
  • Avoid energy drinks — high caffeine plus dehydrating effect is a poor combination for donation day
Drink: more water than usual
  • At least 16 extra ounces of water on donation day, in addition to your normal intake
  • Keep drinking water after you donate — plasma is mostly water and is replenished through hydration
  • Avoid caffeinated and alcoholic beverages as your primary source of fluid on donation day

Why These Rules Exist

Pre-donation nutrition affects your donation in two ways: it affects your eligibility (hemoglobin check) and it affects your comfort (lightheadedness, dizziness).

The hemoglobin check. Before every donation, staff do a quick finger-stick to measure your hemoglobin — the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen. The minimum is 12.5 g/dL for women and 13.0 g/dL for men. If you fall below the cutoff, you cannot donate that day. Your hemoglobin reflects weeks of nutrition, not just that morning's meal. Consistently eating iron-rich food in the week or two before your appointment is more effective than a single iron-heavy meal the day before.

Why fat causes problems. After a high-fat meal, triglycerides (fat particles) enter your bloodstream and can make the plasma appear milky or cloudy — a condition called lipemia. This cloudiness can interfere with the laboratory tests run on your donation. Importantly, the blood may have to be discarded even though you were healthy and willing. Avoiding fatty foods on donation day eliminates this avoidable waste.

Why hydration prevents dizziness. About 55% of your blood volume is plasma, which is mostly water. When you donate, you lose plasma along with the red cells and platelets. If you arrive already dehydrated, the percentage drop in blood volume is proportionally larger, increasing the risk of a vasovagal response — the sudden lightheadedness, nausea, or fainting that some donors experience. Staying well-hydrated before donation is the most effective single step you can take to have an uneventful experience.

Guidance Sources

Dietary recommendations on this page are consistent with pre-donation guidance from the American Red Cross and Vitalant. Lipemia guidance reflects standard blood banking quality practices per AABB. Iron-absorption pairing guidance is based on standard nutritional science published by the National Institutes of Health Office of Dietary Supplements.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should I eat before donating blood?

Eat a full meal two to four hours before your appointment. Focus on iron-rich foods — lean red meat, chicken, fish, beans, lentils, tofu, or iron-fortified cereal — along with complex carbohydrates and a good amount of water. Pair iron-rich foods with vitamin C to increase iron absorption. Avoid high-fat foods on donation day.

What foods should I avoid before donating blood?

Avoid high-fat foods such as fried food, burgers, pizza, chips, or high-fat dairy in the few hours before donating. A large fatty meal raises the fat content of your blood (lipemia), which can make the plasma appear cloudy and may cause the donation to be discarded during testing. Also avoid alcohol for at least 24 hours before donating.

How much water should I drink before donating blood?

Drink at least 16 extra ounces (two cups) of water in the hours before your appointment, in addition to your normal daily intake. Well-hydrated veins are plumper and easier to access. Good hydration also helps prevent lightheadedness during and after donation.

Can I donate blood on an empty stomach?

You should not donate on an empty stomach. Skipping a meal increases your risk of feeling lightheaded or dizzy during or after donation and can cause your blood sugar to drop during the draw. Eat a regular meal two to four hours before your appointment.

Can I drink coffee before donating blood?

One cup of coffee is generally fine, but avoid a lot of caffeine on donation day. Caffeine is a mild diuretic and can contribute to dehydration. Prioritize water over coffee in the hours before donating.

What foods are high in iron that I should eat before donating?

Good iron sources include lean red meat, poultry, fish, beans, lentils, tofu, spinach, iron-fortified cereals, and pumpkin seeds. Pair iron-rich foods with vitamin C (orange juice, tomatoes, bell peppers) to improve absorption. Avoid tea or coffee immediately after an iron-rich meal, as tannins can inhibit iron absorption.

Does what I eat affect my hemoglobin level for donation?

Your hemoglobin on donation day reflects weeks of nutrition, not just what you ate that morning. Building iron stores takes time. Eating iron-rich food consistently in the week or two before your appointment is more effective than a single pre-donation meal. If you frequently run close to the minimum hemoglobin, talk to a nurse at the center about iron supplementation.

Can I drink alcohol before donating blood?

Avoid alcohol for at least 24 hours before donating. Alcohol is a diuretic and dehydrates you, which increases your risk of lightheadedness after donation. Alcohol after donation is similarly discouraged for the rest of the day.
