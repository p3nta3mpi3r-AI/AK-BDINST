Donor Eligibility

Can I Donate Blood with High Blood Pressure?

Short answer: Yes, if your reading is at or below 180/100 on donation day. A diagnosis of hypertension does not disqualify you — nor does taking blood pressure medication. The only threshold that matters is the actual blood pressure reading staff take when you arrive. If your systolic is 180 or lower and your diastolic is 100 or lower, you are eligible to donate, even if your pressure runs high at other times.

When You Can, When to Wait, When You Can't

When you can donate
  • Blood pressure at or below 180/100 mmHg on donation day (measured at the center)
  • You feel well and have no symptoms such as headache, blurred vision, or chest tightness
  • You are taking blood pressure medication — medication is not a disqualifier
  • You have a history of hypertension that is currently under control
When to wait (temporary deferral)
  • Your reading at the center is above 180 systolic or above 100 diastolic — you will be asked to return when it is lower
  • You are experiencing symptoms of high blood pressure: severe headache, visual changes, chest discomfort
  • You started a new blood pressure medication in the last few days and are still adjusting
When you cannot donate
  • Persistent, uncontrolled hypertension consistently above 180/100 that cannot be brought into range
  • Hypertension combined with significant heart disease, recent stroke, or kidney failure (these underlying conditions are separately disqualifying)

Why the 180/100 Threshold Exists

The 180/100 mmHg cutoff is the standard used by the American Red Cross and most U.S. community blood services, including Oklahoma donor centers. It reflects the point at which blood pressure elevation poses meaningful risk during donation.

What happens during donation. When you donate, you lose roughly 470 mL of blood in eight to ten minutes. Your cardiovascular system compensates through mild, automatic adjustments. For most people with well-controlled hypertension, this is handled easily. At very high pressures — above 180/100 — the physiological stress of donation increases the risk of a sudden spike or a vasovagal response, and the combination could be dangerous.

Why medication doesn't disqualify you. Blood pressure medications (ACE inhibitors, beta blockers, calcium channel blockers, diuretics, and others) are specifically designed to bring your pressure into a safe range. If your medication is working — shown by an in-range reading at the center — there is no additional risk from the medication itself. The drugs do not affect the blood you donate or pose any concern for recipients.

White-coat hypertension. Some donors run higher at the center due to nerves. Staff are accustomed to this. Sitting quietly, relaxing, and having your pressure re-checked after a few minutes often brings readings into range. Hydrating well and avoiding caffeine before your visit can help keep your reading lower.

Day-of preparation. Take your regular blood pressure medication as scheduled before donating — do not skip it thinking it will affect your eligibility. Drink at least 16 ounces of water in the hours before your appointment, avoid heavy caffeine, and let staff know about any new medications or symptoms when you check in.

Guidance Sources

The 180/100 mmHg threshold and medication eligibility standards on this page are drawn from American Red Cross eligibility guidelines and AABB standards for blood donor screening. Your final eligibility is determined by the reading taken at the center on donation day.

Frequently Asked Questions

What blood pressure is too high to donate blood?

The American Red Cross and community blood services set the cutoff at a systolic reading above 180 mmHg or a diastolic reading above 100 mmHg. If your reading on donation day is at or below 180/100, you are generally eligible to donate, even if you have a diagnosis of hypertension and take medication for it.

Can I donate blood if I take blood pressure medication?

Yes. Taking blood pressure medication does not disqualify you from donating. The eligibility check is your actual blood pressure reading on the day of donation, not whether you are medicated. As long as your reading is at or below 180/100 mmHg and you feel well, you can donate.

Will my blood pressure be checked at the donor center?

Yes. Staff take your blood pressure as part of the standard pre-donation mini-physical at every visit. The reading at the center — not a reading from home or a pharmacy — determines whether you can donate that day.

What happens if my blood pressure is too high when I arrive?

If your reading is above 180/100 mmHg, staff will ask you to sit quietly for a few minutes and may re-check. If it remains elevated, you will be deferred for that visit. This is a temporary deferral — once your blood pressure is controlled, you can return and try again.

Does donating blood lower blood pressure?

Some observational studies suggest that regular blood donation may be associated with modest reductions in blood pressure over time, but blood donation is not a treatment for hypertension. Do not donate specifically hoping to lower your blood pressure. The primary goal of donation is to help patients who need blood transfusions.

Can I donate if my blood pressure is controlled but I have had a hypertensive crisis in the past?

A past hypertensive crisis does not automatically disqualify you permanently. What matters is your blood pressure reading on donation day. If your pressure is at or below 180/100 mmHg and you feel well, you are generally eligible. If you have ongoing heart disease or organ damage from past hypertensive episodes, discuss your situation with screening staff.

Does high blood pressure affect the blood I donate?

No. Well-controlled high blood pressure does not compromise the safety or usability of your donated blood for transfusion recipients. The eligibility threshold is set for your safety as a donor, not because of concerns about blood quality.
