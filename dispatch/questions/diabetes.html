Donor Eligibility

Can I Donate Blood with Diabetes?

Short answer: Yes, in most cases. If your diabetes — Type 1 or Type 2 — is well-controlled and you feel well on donation day, you can donate blood under U.S. guidelines. The key caveats: your blood sugar must be stable (no recent medication changes in the last four weeks, no recent hypoglycemic episodes requiring help), and active diabetic complications such as kidney disease or foot ulcers under treatment are disqualifying.

When You Can, When to Wait, When You Can't

When you can donate
  • Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes, either well-controlled by diet, oral medication, or insulin
  • Blood sugar is stable and in your normal range on donation day
  • You feel well — no dizziness, shakiness, or signs of hypoglycemia
  • No insulin type or dose change in the past four weeks
  • No serious diabetic complications currently under active treatment
When to wait
  • Your insulin type or dose changed in the last four weeks — wait until blood sugar has been stable for at least four weeks
  • You had a hypoglycemic episode in the last few weeks that required assistance from another person — wait until you are episode-free and stable
  • You are not feeling well on the day you plan to donate
When you cannot donate
  • Active foot ulcers or wounds currently under medical treatment
  • Significant diabetic kidney disease (nephropathy)
  • Severe peripheral neuropathy affecting your safety or ability to donate
  • Diabetes that is not well-controlled regardless of type or medication

Why These Rules Exist

Blood donation eligibility rules for people with diabetes are designed to protect both the donor and the person who receives the blood.

Donor safety comes first. The donation process draws about 470 mL of whole blood over eight to ten minutes. For someone with well-controlled diabetes, this is well-tolerated. But if your blood sugar is already unstable — from a recent medication change or a recent low episode — the mild physical stress of donation could trigger a hypoglycemic event at the donation center. The four-week stability window gives your body time to adjust to new insulin dosing before adding that stress.

Recipient safety matters too. There is no evidence that blood donated by a person with well-controlled diabetes poses any risk to the recipient. Diabetes is a metabolic condition, not a bloodborne pathogen. The blood itself is tested extensively regardless of donor health history. The eligibility rules focus on whether donation is safe for you, not on concerns about the blood quality from a diabetic donor.

Why active complications disqualify you. Conditions like diabetic kidney disease or active foot ulcers signal that your diabetes may not be fully under control, or that your body is managing significant physiological stress. Donating in that state increases your risk of an adverse reaction. These are not permanent disqualifications — if your complications resolve and your condition stabilizes, speak with a screening nurse about re-evaluating your eligibility.

U.S. vs. UK guidelines. If you have read that insulin-dependent diabetics cannot donate blood, that guidance comes from the UK's NHS, which takes a stricter approach. U.S. organizations including the American Red Cross, Vitalant, and Oklahoma donor centers follow guidelines set by the AABB and FDA that permit insulin-dependent donors provided they meet the stability criteria above. Follow U.S. rules when donating in Oklahoma.

Guidance Sources

This page reflects eligibility standards published by the American Red Cross and aligned with AABB and FDA donor screening guidelines. Individual donor eligibility is always confirmed during the on-site health history interview. Staff at any Oklahoma donor center can answer questions specific to your situation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Type 1 diabetics donate blood?

Yes. People with Type 1 diabetes can donate blood in the United States as long as their diabetes is well-controlled, they feel well on the day of donation, and they have not had a hypoglycemic episode requiring assistance from another person in the past few weeks. This follows American Red Cross and AABB guidelines. Note that UK (NHS) guidelines are stricter and exclude insulin-dependent donors — U.S. centers follow the more permissive U.S. standard.

Can Type 2 diabetics donate blood?

Yes. Type 2 diabetics can donate blood provided their condition is well-controlled — whether managed by diet alone, oral medication, or insulin — and they are feeling well on donation day. There is no blanket exclusion for Type 2 diabetes under U.S. donor eligibility guidelines.

What blood sugar level is safe before donating blood?

U.S. blood services do not publish a specific numeric cutoff for pre-donation blood glucose. The standard is that your diabetes must be well-controlled and stable. If you regularly monitor your glucose, a reading in your normal range on donation day is a practical guide. Staff at the donor center will ask health screening questions and can advise you.

Do I need to disclose my diabetes to the donor center?

Yes. Every donor completes a health history questionnaire before donating. You will be asked about medical conditions including diabetes and any medications you take. Honest answers protect both you and the recipient. Screening staff will confirm your eligibility based on your answers.

Can I donate plasma if I have diabetes?

Source plasma donation (through apheresis programs) generally follows the same eligibility standards as whole blood for diabetes. Well-controlled diabetes is not an automatic exclusion. However, specific plasma programs may have additional requirements, so confirm with the collection center before your appointment.

Can I donate if I recently changed my insulin dose?

If your insulin type or dose has changed in the last four weeks, you should wait until your blood sugar has been stable for at least four weeks before donating. Recent dosage changes mean your glucose control may not yet be fully established, which could put you at risk during donation.

Does donating blood affect blood sugar levels?

Donating a standard unit of blood (about 470 mL) can temporarily affect fluid balance. Eating a good meal and drinking extra fluids before and after donation helps stabilize your glucose. Follow any guidance from your diabetes care team if you have specific concerns about glucose management on donation day.

Can I donate if I have diabetic complications like kidney disease or foot ulcers?

Active diabetic complications — such as foot ulcers currently under treatment, significant kidney disease, or severe peripheral nerve damage — are generally disqualifying. These conditions affect your safety as a donor and may indicate that your diabetes is not fully controlled. Speak with screening staff if you are unsure about your specific situation.
